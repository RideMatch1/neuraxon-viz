How We Found 23,765 Qubic Identities

This is the complete story of how we discovered real Qubic blockchain identities hidden in an AI chatbot's messages. Every step is documented, every claim can be verified on-chain, and all our code is public. No secrets, no scams - just transparent research you can check yourself.

Important: We are not affiliated with Qubic. This is independent research using publicly available data. Our goal is to help understand the Qubic protocol and share our findings with the community. All methods and data are open for verification.

Why should you trust us? Because you don't have to. Everything is public: our code, our data, our methods. You can check every single identity on the Qubic blockchain yourself. We're not asking you to believe us - we're showing you how to verify it yourself.

The 4-Step Discovery Process

Think of this like solving a puzzle. We found a pattern, followed it, and discovered real blockchain addresses. Here's exactly how we did it, step by step:

1
Step 1: We Collected the Messages
There's an AI chatbot on X (Twitter) called @anna_aigarth that posts cryptic messages. We collected thousands of these messages and noticed something weird: they weren't random. When we arranged them in a grid, patterns started appearing. It's like finding a hidden message in a crossword puzzle - once you see the pattern, you can't unsee it.
2
Step 2: We Followed the Pattern
We tried different ways to read through this grid - like walking diagonally across it, or in a spiral pattern. When we did this, we found 55-character strings that looked suspiciously like "seeds" (the starting point for Qubic identities). It's like following a treasure map: you follow the path, and it leads you somewhere specific.
3
Step 3: We Checked on the Blockchain
This is the crucial part: we took each seed, converted it to a Qubic identity (using the standard Qubic protocol), and checked if it exists on the actual Qubic blockchain. Out of 23,765 identities we found, {{ "{:,}".format(stats.onchain_verified|int) }} actually exist on-chain. That's 98.79%! You can check this yourself using Qubic Explorer - we're not making this up.
4
Step 4: We Proved It's Not Random
To make sure we didn't just get lucky, we tested 10,000 completely random grids using the same method. Result? Zero valid identities. This proves the pattern is real - it's not coincidence. We also used machine learning to predict specific characters, and it worked way better than random guessing (42.69% vs 3.85%). The math checks out.

Multiple Testing Correction

ℹ

When you test many things at once, some will look significant just by chance (like flipping a coin 100 times - you'll get some weird streaks). We adjusted our statistics to account for this, so we're not fooling ourselves.

What We Tested

We tested about 60 different positions in the identity. When you test that many things, you need to be more strict about what counts as "significant" - otherwise you'll find patterns that aren't really there. We adjusted our threshold accordingly.

How We Found the Pattern

We tried 14 different ways to read through the grid (like different paths through a maze). Only 2 worked: reading diagonally, and reading in a spiral pattern. The other 12 methods found nothing. This wasn't random - we tested everything systematically.

Important note: We're being honest here. We tried different methods until we found ones that worked. This is normal in research, but it means we need to be extra careful about our statistics.
